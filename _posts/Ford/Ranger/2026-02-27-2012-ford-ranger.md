# 2012 Ford Ranger

### Overview
The 2012 Ford Ranger, a compact pickup truck, generally presented a solid, albeit basic, mechanical package for its era. Known for its utilitarian design and robust build, it appealed to those seeking a no-frills workhorse. While not without its quirks, the Ranger's reliability is often considered good for its class, with many owners reporting long service lives when properly maintained. Common areas of attention tend to be related to its age and mileage, with some specific components showing a higher propensity for issues than others. This analysis focuses on documented technical reliability concerns beyond standard maintenance.

## Electrical

### General Electrical System
**Alternator Failure**
- **Symptoms:** Dimming lights, battery warning light, difficulty starting, complete loss of electrical power.
- **Solution:** Replace alternator.
- **Cost:** $300 - $600

**Window Regulator Failure**
- **Symptoms:** Power windows not operating, window stuck in open or closed position.
- **Solution:** Replace window regulator assembly.
- **Cost:** $200 - $400


## Exterior

### Body and Frame
**Rust on Frame and Body Panels**
- **Symptoms:** Visible corrosion, bubbling paint, structural weakness, especially in areas with harsh winters or high humidity.
- **Solution:** Rust treatment, panel replacement, or frame repair.
- **Cost:** $500 - $2000+

### Summary Verdict
The 2012 Ford Ranger is a generally reliable compact truck, particularly for its powertrain components. The engines and transmissions offered for this model year are known for their durability. The most common reported issues tend to be related to age and environmental factors, such as rust on the frame and body, and some electrical components like the alternator and window regulators can fail over time. These are not necessarily indicative of inherent design flaws but rather typical wear and tear for a vehicle of this age. With proactive maintenance and addressing rust concerns, the 2012 Ranger can remain a dependable vehicle.
